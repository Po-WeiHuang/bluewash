# Executive Summary

## Giant Kelp: A Validated Marine Carbon Dioxide Removal Pathway

As the world seeks new approaches to address climate change, Marine Carbon Dioxide Removal (mCDR) strategies are receiving increased attention. Among these strategies, kelp cultivation offers significant potential for scalable carbon sequestration: kelp grows quickly, draws down carbon dioxide, and offers many environmental co-benefits. Despite this promise, kelp-based carbon removal has faced skepticism regarding measurement accuracy, permanence of sequestration, and additionality—key considerations in carbon credit markets.

**This whitepaper presents a breakthrough in the measurement, reporting, and verification (MRV) of kelp-based carbon dioxide removal.** Through an independent collaboration between <u>atdepth</u> (marine modelling and MRV technology), <u>Kelp Blue</u> (kelp cultivation), and the <u>Kelp Forest Foundation</u> (kelp research and innovation nonprofit), we have developed and implemented a rigorous methodology that establishes kelp cultivation as a viable pathway for atmospheric carbon dioxide removal with multi-century permanence.

The results affirm that kelp cultivation creates measurable, additional atmospheric CO₂ removal; 150 tons of CO2 in 2024 according to the calculations, and without considering the harvested kelp. Based on the *average mature area* under cultivation throughout 2024, this corresponds to a carbon sequestration potential of approximately 16 - 25 tons per ha. This breakthrough has profound implications for marine CDR, establishing kelp cultivation as a valid carbon removal pathway that can be mechanistically measured and verified to the same rigorous standards as technological approaches. Perhaps most significantly, the implemented methodology clearly distinguishes between carbon in harvested biomass and carbon that enters permanent sequestration pathways, enabling kelp farms to produce both commercial products and verified carbon removal without double-counting or compromising environmental integrity.

# The need for nature-based mCDR solutions and the potential of kelp cultivation

Climate change has long reached a point where reducing emissions is no longer enough – we also need to actively remove carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. The ocean, already the world’s largest carbon sink, holds enormous potential to contribute to carbon removal.

Marine Carbon Dioxide Removal (mCDR) is a fast-growing field that explores how ocean-based interventions can help lock carbon away. Examples include ocean alkalinity enhancement, direct ocean capture, and macroalgae cultivation. Among them, kelp cultivation is increasingly recognized as a promising mCDR pathway. **Kelp, and particularly giant kelp (*Macrocystis pyrifera*), grows quickly, absorbing vast amounts of CO₂, and brings added benefits for biodiversity and local economies.** What has been missing, until now, is the ability to quantify kelp’s contribution to durable carbon removal with the same rigor expected of technological CDR solutions.

This whitepaper introduces a breakthrough in measurement, verification and reporting (MRV) that answers those questions with clarity and confidence. With this methodology, kelp cultivation can now be positioned as a next-generation blue carbon strategy and a validated mCDR solution.

# Glossary

**Blue Carbon and Marine CDR: Understanding the Landscape**
Blue Carbon traditionally refers to carbon captured and stored in coastal and marine ecosystems like mangroves, salt marshes, and seagrass meadows, but increasingly includes seaweed. These ecosystems sequester carbon in their biomass and sediments, but also in the ocean via organic carbon sequestration.

**Where Kelp Cultivation Fits:**
Kelp cultivation represents a unique intersection of traditional blue carbon approaches and engineered mCDR solutions. Kelp-based sequestration leverages the ocean’s vast depth and circulation patterns to achieve permanence. And unlike some proposed mCDR interventions that introduce materials to the ocean or consume significant energy, kelp cultivation works by enhancing natural biological processes with minimal ecological disruption, and significant co-benefits.

**Marine Carbon Dioxide Removal**
(mCDR) encompasses a broader range of interventions designed to enhance the ocean’s natural carbon sink function.

These approaches include:

**> Ocean Alkalinity Enhancement:**
Adding alkaline materials to increase the ocean’s capacity to absorb and store CO₂

**> Direct Ocean Capture:**
Directly extracting CO₂ that is dissolved in seawater, then storing the extracted CO₂ geologically or utilizing it

**> Ocean Iron Fertilization:**
Adding iron to stimulate phytoplankton growth and carbon uptake

**> Macroalgae Cultivation:**
Growing seaweed like kelp that captures carbon through photosynthesis

# The case study: scalable solutions from Namibia’s Southern coast

![A split-level view showing a boat on the surface of the water and a dense forest of giant kelp growing beneath the surface.](image)

# Lüderitz, Namibia:

Kelp Blue cultivates giant kelp near Lüderitz, along the southern coastline of Namibia. This region, influenced by the cold, nutrient-rich waters of the Benguela current, provides ideal conditions for kelp cultivation. At the farm, giant kelp is grown on lines anchored to the seafloor, forming an underwater forest that mimics the structure and function of natural kelp ecosystems. As the kelp grows, it forms a dense canopy on the ocean surface. Kelp Blue repeat harvests this canopy to produce a biostimulant: an agricultural input that enhances crop health and resilience to heat, drought and salinity stress. The kelp biomass that remains unharvested naturally contributes to carbon sequestration by entering long-term storage pathways in the ocean.

By the end of 2024, Kelp Blue had installed 30 hectares of kelp farm, with juvenile kelp gradually maturing over several months before becoming visible at the surface - with an expected 5x increase in sequestration next year as the installed cultivation area matures.

In many parts of the world, natural kelp forests are in decline due to pollution, overharvesting, and habitat loss, limiting their role in oceanic carbon capture. By contrast, Kelp Blue’s offshore farms provide a scalable, regenerative alternative. **Cultivated kelp not only increases the carbon-capturing capacity in the ocean but also delivers sustainable products that reduce reliance on synthetic agricultural inputs or other fossil-fuel based products like plastics.** This case study demonstrates how cultivated kelp can serve both ecological and commercial purposes while delivering measurable climate benefits.

# Carbon Pathways in Kelp Cultivation

In the context of Kelp Blue’s giant kelp farm in Lüderitz, the methodology identified two primary carbon sequestration pathways:

**Dissolved organic carbon (DOC)** Export: Kelp naturally exudes dissolved organic carbon. A portion of this DOC is “refractory”, meaning it resists degradation and remains in the ocean for hundreds to thousands of years. Additionally, when DOC is exported to the deep ocean (below 1000m), it remains sequestered on century timescales due to ocean circulation patterns.

**Particulate organic carbon (POC)** Export: As kelp grows and erodes, it sheds particulate material (containing carbon stored in the tissue). This material can be transported by ocean currents and settle to the deep ocean or buried in sediments in the continental shelf, effectively sequestering carbon for centuries or longer.

By mechanistically tracking these two natural sequestration pathways and comparing them to a counterfactual scenario without kelp cultivation, we can precisely quantify the additional carbon sequestration attributable to kelp farming operations, and the corresponding atmospheric removal.

# Addressing Key Misconceptions

1. **“Kelp carbon sequestration isn’t permanent”**
   **Finding:** Our methodology identifies multiple permanent sequestration pathways supported by scientific literature. Carbon exported to the deep ocean (below 1000m) remains sequestered for centuries due to ocean circulation timescales. Additionally, a significant fraction of kelp-derived DOC becomes refractory, resisting degradation for millennia. Kelp-derived carbon buried in sediments also achieves multi-century permanence.

2. **“We can’t directly measure ocean carbon sequestration”**
   **Finding:** Our methodology combines real-world monitoring with state-of-the-art ocean modeling that resolves all relevant physical and biogeochemical processes across appropriate spatial and temporal scales. By comparing modeled scenarios with and without kelp, we precisely quantify the additional carbon sequestration and atmospheric carbon dioxide removal attributable to kelp farming.

3. **“Harvested kelp cannot be counted as carbon removal”**
   **Finding:** Our carbon accounting clearly differentiates between harvested biomass and carbon sequestered through natural ocean processes. Credits are based only on the portion of carbon that enters long-term ocean sequestration pathways, not harvested biomass. This enables kelp farmers to produce two distinct products without double-counting:
   (1) harvested kelp biomass for commercial applications,
   (2) carbon dioxide removal credits from the portion of kelp production that naturally enters oceanic sequestration.

# 4 “Profitable carbon projects cannot be additional”

**Finding:** Financial viability and additionality are distinct concepts. Our approach defines additionality based on the physical impact of the kelp farm on carbon flows, not its economic profile. The farm’s carbon sequestration is additional because it would not have occurred without the intervention, regardless of the economic drivers behind that intervention. Indeed, the ability to generate both commercial products and carbon removal creates a powerful scaling incentive that can accelerate climate impact. The carbon credits generated are verified to represent genuinely additional atmospheric CO₂ removal, while the commercial biostimulant revenue enables rapid scaling without relying solely on carbon markets—an advantage for accelerating climate action.

# 5 “Kelp farming doesn’t lead to additional atmospheric CO2 uptake by the ocean”

**Finding:** As for carbon sequestration, comparing modeled kelp and no-kelp scenarios allows us to quantify the additional atmospheric carbon dioxide removal attributable to kelp farming. Our work mechanistically shows that the Lüderitz kelp farm altered local carbonate chemistry in ways that enhanced air-sea gas exchange, directly drawing down atmospheric CO₂ at rates that exceed what would occur in the counterfactual scenario (i.e. in the absence of the kelp farm).

# Technical Methodology Overview

Kelp farms and kelp ecosystems operate within a complex and constantly changing environment. Currents, temperature, salinity, depth, and ocean chemistry all influence how carbon is absorbed, transformed, and either stored or returned to the atmosphere.

Oceanographic models can mechanistically resolve many of the physical, chemical, and biological processes that determine carbon movement in marine environments. Detailed simulations are made possible because the fundamental equations governing ocean physics, biogeochemistry, and fluid dynamics are well established and can be solved computationally at relevant scales. This means our modeling approach doesn’t merely estimate or approximate carbon flows—it models them based on first principles with high spatial and temporal resolution and enables their validation using monitoring data. **This fundamental distinction provides significantly greater certainty in quantifying marine carbon sequestration compared to other carbon accounting approaches for mCDR, blue carbon and certain land-based pathways that often rely on statistical approximations.**

14

atdepth’s approach represents a complete modeling system that mirrors real-world ocean dynamics with a high level of detail, integrating:

### Physical Ocean Modeling

* High-resolution hydrodynamic modeling of ocean currents, mixing, and transport
* Boundary conditions informed by global ocean reanalysis products
* Realistic representation of coastal processes and bathymetry

### Biogeochemical Modeling

* Comprehensive carbon chemistry and nutrient cycling modeling
* Kelp growth dynamics calibrated to site-specific conditions and informed by monitoring data
* Air-sea gas exchange dynamics

### Carbon Sequestration and Removal Quantification

* Dissolved organic carbon export to the deep ocean and refractory pool formation
* Particulate organic carbon transport, sinking, and sediment burial
* Additional atmospheric carbon dioxide uptake calculation through air-sea gas exchange model

### Monitoring and Validation

* Remote and aerial sensing of kelp canopy extent
* In-situ measurements of oceanographic conditions
* Calibration of model parameters to observed conditions

# Future research priorities

While our methodology represents a breakthrough in kelp-based carbon removal MRV, we have identified key areas for continued research to further enhance measurement precision and reduce remaining uncertainties. These areas for continued research would allow applicability for other regions and kelp species.

1. **DOC Characterization and Sequestration Refinement:** the exact amount of DOC that becomes refractory can vary by species, growing conditions, and location. We need site-specific measurements of refractory DOC fraction and exudation rates to reduce uncertainty ranges. <u>Because refractory DOC likely accounts for a significant fraction of carbon sequestered from kelp-based interventions</u>, improving our understanding of its behaviour is top research priority.

2. **Enhanced 3D Biomass Monitoring:** To improve accuracy of biomass below the surface, we need <u>better ways of measuring kelp growth throughout its entire vertical structure.</u> Development of cost-effective and scalable depth-resolving biomass monitoring is needed to improve growth model inputs.

3. **POC Fate Characterization:** <u>Direct measurements of erosion rates and sediment burial efficiency</u> to refine particulate carbon sequestration quantification. Improved empirical data on long-term POC pathways will enhance the accuracy of carbon accounting and help calibrate biogeochemical model components.

# Implications for Carbon Markets

This methodology establishes kelp farming as a credible carbon dioxide removal pathway that meets the rigorous standards expected in high-quality carbon markets:

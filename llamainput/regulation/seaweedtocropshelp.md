### A B S T R A C T
The ecological significance, economic worth, and unrealized potential of seaweeds in a variety of industries, including agriculture, are becoming more widely acknowledged. A few products obtained from Seaweeds are known for their biostimulant activities such as enhancing growth and yield of horticultural crops. In this review, the phytocompounds such as polysaccharides, betaines, phenols, carotene etc. contained in the seaweed extract are profiled and their impact on vegetables and fruits are enlisted. The different source of seaweed extracts i.e. brown, green and red seaweed on productivity and quality of crops are explored. The overview brought to light the presence of various plant growth regulators, vitamins, minerals and other bioactive compounds in seaweed extracts that are utilized in different aspects of crop production including stress tolerance and nutrient enhancement. Studies on the qualitative and quantitative analysis of these three different sources in plant and soil application revealed that the brown seaweed extract performs well on growth and yield for sustainable agriculture.

### Introduction

Plant biostimulant is a substance or microorganism that is applied to plants in order to increase nutrient efficiency, tolerance to abiotic stress, and crop quality [1]. Crop growth is affected by various factors and, farmers are not getting proper yield and quality due to the usage of different types of chemicals and fertilizers. In the current scenario, organic horticulture is an environmentally-friendly production system, able to produce food with marginal harm to ecosystems (i.e., water and soil conservation) by using limited off-farm inputs [2]. They have steadily grown in the agricultural industry over the last decade, positioning itself as one of the key emerging tactics for increasing crop production and resilience to climate change. Furthermore, plant biostimulants from natural sources have received substantial consideration by both the scientific community and commercial enterprises especially in the past two decades. Besides, it helps in offering a potentially innovative approach for the regulation/modification of physiological processes in plants to encourage growth, to mitigate stress-induced limitations, and to increase yield [3].

Major group of plant biostimulants are humic acid and fulvic acid, protein hydrolysates and other N-containing compounds, seaweed extracts and botanicals, chitosan and other biopolymers, inorganic compounds, beneficial fungi and bacteria [4]. Apart from non-microbial biostimulants, microbial biostimulants has been used in agriculture in recent times. Microbial inoculants mostly comprise plant growth promoting rhizobacteria (PGPR) and endophytic fungi such as Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi and *Trichoderma* spp. have been used as biostimulants [5].

Agricultural and horticultural crops typically necessitate a large number of fertilizers and pesticides. Higher nutrient availability may not always translate into higher quality products. On the contrary, disproportionate fertilizer application, and particularly high nitrogen supply, encourages vegetative growth with a higher susceptibility to pathogens [6]. Non-microbial and microbial plant biostimulants may positively impact nutrient use efficiency (NUE), nitrogen (N) in particular, by enhancing root system architecture and soil exploration in addition to increasing macro- and micronutrient solubilization that can result in an increase in NUE [7].

A few products obtained from Seaweeds include, Agar, Alginic acid, Carrageenan (extracted from red seaweeds like Gigartinaceae, Soliriaceae, and Hypneaceae), mannitol (brown algae), Liquid seaweed fertilizer (LSF) and animal feed are commonly reported in the literature. In line with this, it is also possible to acquire and formulate novel nature-based biostimulants from seaweed extracts to promote vegetable and fruit production, which provide an alternative to agrochemicals. In general, most of the agrochemicals cause detrimental effects to soil and water environments besides affecting the health of human population. If seaweed extract has been used as an alternative source of nutrient to crop plants than chemical fertilizers, farmers can still meet their higher production. This ensures the organic production of vegetables and fruits. This review was carried out with an objective of phytocompounds present in different seaweed extracts through the literature search and to focus on the role and mechanism of bioactive compounds acting on different vegetable and fruit crop production.

## Seaweed extract

Seaweeds are macroscopic marine algae which are used as human food, cattle feed etc., which is a substitute to chemical fertilizer and/or chemical compounds. There are more than 20,000 species of marine plants worldwide. Seaweeds are a valuable source of phycocolloids like agar, alginate, and carrageenan. They also serve as food, fodder, feed, and liquid fertilizer [8]. Globally, around 7.5 - 8.0 million kilograms of wet seaweeds are collected annually. In India, the primary commercial uses of seaweed are to produce the compounds agar and alginate [9]. Seaweed extracts serve as organic nutrient supplements, biostimulants, or bio-fertilizers, offering a natural alternative to chemical fertilizers in farming and gardening to enhance plant growth and productivity [10]. In addition, seaweeds and their extracts improve soil health by increasing the soil's ability to retain moisture and by encouraging the development of helpful soil microorganisms. Seaweeds have an influence on agricultural crops by stimulating the growth of plants, seedlings, root hair and secondary roots. Moreover, it can enhance fruit setting, nutrient incorporation, pest and disease resistance and stress management (drought, salinity and temperature) [11]. Seaweed extracts are marketed as liquid fertilizers and biostimulants because they encompass multiple growth promoting substances such as cytokinins, auxins, gibberellins and other macro and micro nutrients required for plant growth and development [12].

## Types of seaweed and their chemical composition

Seaweeds are grouped into three main divisions such as Chlorophyceae (green algae), Phaeophyceae (brown algae) and Rhodophyceae (red algae) (Table 1 and Fig. 1) [13]. This classification relies on factors such as colour, cellular composition and various other characteristics of the seaweed.

On perusal of the above, it is brought to understanding that polysaccharides, sterols, polyphenolics, carotenoids, and lipids are various components that exist in algae species (Table 2 and Fig. 2). Each type of algae synthesizes specific polysaccharides tailored to its cellular structure and ecological niche. For example, brown algae comprise cell walls with alginate and sulfated fucoidans, while red algae synthesize sulfated galactans like agar and carrageenan. Green algae, in contrast, produce glucans and xylans. The dominant sterols in algae are influenced by taxonomic differences. Brown algae contain fucosterol in abundance, while red algae have cholesterol derivatives, and green algae are rich in phytosterols, such as ergosterol. Brown algae are unique in producing phlorotannins, which provide protection against environmental stressors like UV radiation. Bromophenols, found across all algal groups, deter herbivory. Green algae utilize flavonoids for various purposes, such as growth and defence. Brown algae contain fucoxanthin pigments, which aid in photosynthesis in the marine environment. Red and green algae produce carotenoids, such as lutein and violaxanthin, which serve light-protective and antioxidant functions. The different types of lipids reflect taxonomic and habitat variations. Red and green algae have glycolipids that relate to the structural properties of chloroplast membranes, while glycerolipids and phospholipids vary to suit the marine (brown algae) or fresh water (green algae) environment.

### Role of seaweed bioactive compounds

Polysaccharides are the main compound that makes up seaweed. The chemical structures of seaweed polysaccharides vary depending on the species, taxonomic group, season of harvest and extraction method. Agar, obtained from red seaweed such as *Gracilaria gracilis* and *Asparagopsis armata*, boosts plant growth and seed germination [15]. They are helpful in preventing pathogens by increased production of secondary metabolites and helps in eliciting plant defense mechanism. They are good in reducing both biotic and abiotic stress. Seaweed provide higher germination rate, increased plant vigor and nutrient uptake [15]. Seaweed polysaccharides result in peaked growth parameters in comparison with chemical fertilizer [38]. Germination of seeds are excellent by treating seeds with quaternary ammonium seaweed polysaccharide salts [39]. It offers abundant natural phenolic compounds with potential applications in agriculture, enhancing plant growth and resilience to stress. These compounds may boost plant immunity and shield against various stresses, both environmental and biological [40]. Betaines have been discovered in a range of seaweed species from Chlorophyta, Heterokontophyta and Rhodophyta, suggesting their extensive distribution among marine algae [41]. Betaines like glycine betaine, gamma-aminobutyric acid betaine, d-aminovaleric acid betaine and laminine, found in seaweed biomass and extracts, serve as osmolytes, improving plant tolerance to high temperature, drought, and salinity [42].

### Distribution and components

Owing to their distribution and abundance, brown seaweeds (Phaeophyta) are among the most widely used extracts for use in horticulture and agriculture on a commercial basis. *Ascophyllum nodosum*, *Ecklonia maxima*, *Macrocystis pyrifera* and *Durvillea potatorum* are the brown seaweeds, that is most frequently used for commercial purposes [43]. Fucoidans and alginates are two polyuronides, abundantly present in brown seaweeds [44]. Phytohormones such as auxins, gibberellins, cytokinin, abscisic acid and brassinosteroids are abundant in SWE extract derived from *Ascophyllum nodosum* [45]. *Ascophyllum nodosum* is a marine plant that thrives in temperate aquatic environments, specifically in the Atlantic and Arctic seas. This plant has been extensively researched due to its various attributes, such as plant growth promotion and being utilized in animal feed [46]. When applied on crops like fruits, vegetables and flowers, notable enhancements have been observed, including improved yields, enhanced absorption of soil nutrients, heightened resilience against certain pests like red spider mites and aphids, improved seed germination and increased resistance to frost in plants [47]. The incorporation of powdered seaweeds in limited quantities into soil has proven to be an effective form of fertilizer for cultivating vegetable crops. The introduction of seaweed into the soil has been found to enhance the development of lateral roots, leaves, leaflets and overall lamina growth in plants [48].

### Comparative analysis of effects of different seaweed extracts on crops

#### a) *Solanum tuberosum*

Spraying 10% *Kappaphycus alvarezii* sap (K sap) directly onto the leaves, in addition to the prescribed fertilizer regimen, led to notable improvements in plant height, rate of tuber formation and overall tuber yield, including an increase in yield enhancement [49]. The sap acts on root to modify the root architecture which increases the forage area for better nutrient uptake. Seaweed extract contain phytohormones which helps in overall plant growth and enhancing phytochemical compounds [49]. The most successful application was observed with spraying *Kappaphycus alvarezii* sap at a concentration of 7.5% combined with 100% of the recommended dose of fertilizer (RDF). This combination resulted in the highest yield and optimal growth characteristics [50]. The presence of phosphorous, auxin and potassium helps in root growth, enhanced root shoot ratio, biomass accumulation, maintenance of crop quality like increased photosynthesis, ATP synthesis and disease resistance [51].

The foliar use of seaweed extract "Primo" markedly enhanced the growth, yield and quality of potato plants. The highest potato yield occurred when the seaweed extract was applied at intervals of 30 and 60 days after planting [52]. Using 10% G sap along with the recommended fertilizer dosage, as well as employing 10% K sap with the recommended fertilizer dosage, also boosted both the marketable and total tuber yield of potatoes [53]. Bio-algeen S90 (*Ascophyllum nodosum*) and Kelpak SL (*Ecklonia maxima*) consistently yield better results across all growing seasons, averaging around 33.19 t/ha and 33.4 t/ha, respectively [54]. ### Effect of seaweed extract on plant growth

Seaweed extracts have been studied extensively for potential use in crop production to improve biomass yield and quality of the produce. These extracts have shown improvement in seed germination and plant growth at all stages, ranging from harvest and post-harvest [14]. Seed priming (controlled hydration) with seaweed extract increases seed germination compared to priming with water. It has also resulted in greater antioxidant effects in spinach [71]. Marine seaweed, rich in amalgam, a novel bioactive component helps in increasing seed germination as well as biochemical components viz., phenol content, antioxidant activity etc. in *Capsicum annum* [72].

Foliar spray and soil application of seaweed extract has its unique role in both plant protection and plant growth promotion. It excels well than chemical fertilizer due to its properties such as bio-degradable, non-toxic, eco-friendly which makes it more preferable for sustainable agriculture and organic farming. Further, seaweed plays a major role in seedling growth, development of root hair and secondary roots, nutrient incorporation, larger number of fruit setting and shows resistance among pests and diseases [73].

Nematode parasites can lead to substantial infestation and harm to plants. Nonetheless, studies demonstrate that the utilization of seaweed extracts has been effective in diminishing nematode infestations in sunflower [79] and tomato [80].

Nevertheless, the nematicidal activity primarily constitutes a component of the plant defence mechanism, potentially through adjustments in the cytokinin: auxin ratio. This is evident from the fact that seaweed extracts themselves do not possess direct nematicidal properties [81]. Furthermore, the incidence of citrus greening was decreased by reducing the population of *Diaphorina citri* through the application of extracts from *Caulerpa sertularioides*, *Laurencia johnstonii* and *Sargassum horridum* [82].

Seaweed extracts also function as inducers for plant defence mechanisms against detrimental bacterial, fungal and viral pathogens, thereby safeguarding crops from significant economic losses due to diseases [4,83]. The use of seaweed extracts effectively managed various fungal and bacterial diseases. The decline in infection levels can be attributed to the overall enhancement of plant vigour after treatment with seaweed extracts, pre-existing resistance, induced systemic or systemic acquired resistance, or improved soil suppressiveness resulting from changes in microbial dynamics [14]. In addition to triggering defence mechanisms against bacterial and fungal pathogens, seaweed extracts have demonstrated the ability to manage severe symptoms caused by plant viruses. The symptoms of tomato chlorotic dwarf viroid were notably reduced when pre-treated with a $\lambda$-carrageenan polysaccharide derived from seaweed extracts. Likewise, the severity of tobacco mosaic virus (TMV) was significantly diminished through the application of sulfated galactans, a prominent component found in certain seaweed extracts [16].

### *Influence of seaweed extract on abiotic stress management*

Beyond the challenges posed by pests and diseases, crop productivity can also be hindered by diverse environmental stresses like drought, elevated temperatures, salinity and freezing conditions. Predictions indicate that around 50% of arable lands could be affected by high salt and drought conditions by the year 2050. These abiotic stresses may result in the accumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), ultimately causing harm to the plant system [84,85]. In specific, plants subjected to seaweed extracts like *Ascophyllum nodosum* and *Sargassum* spp. exhibited resilience against the detrimental impacts of abiotic stresses. For example, application of seaweed extracts resulted in a notable decrease in leaf osmotic potential in grapevines and tomato, thereby averting extensive damage [86]. Seaweed extract, particularly from *Ascophyllum nodosum*, alleviated heat stress in spinach seeds by increasing antioxidant metabolism, germination and seedling vigour.

Seaweed extracts have higher amounts of minerals, amino acids and plant hormones, which boost antioxidant activity and counteract stress-induced oxidative damage. These extracts may also increase repair mechanisms in membranes and DNA systems, resulting in better seed physiological performance under extreme temperatures [71]. The application of seaweed extracts to Faba bean increases biomass production during drought stress by increasing osmo protectants such as proline and soluble sugars, improving water content, diminishing lipid peroxidation, and increasing phenol levels. Seaweed extracts reduce drought stress by scavenging reactive oxygen species (ROS), increasing antioxidant synthesis, improving photosynthetic efficiency and reducing lipid peroxidation. Seaweed extracts promote proline accumulation, which aids in osmotic adjustment and membrane protection, whereas higher soluble sugar levels help with ROS scavenging, membrane protection, and osmotic adjustment during drought stress [87]. ## Conclusion

Seaweed extracts are natural and environment friendly, thus they are used as good source of bio-stimulants for increased crop production. The three types of seaweed extract viz., brown, green and red seaweed extracts, brown seaweed extract excels as the most preferred seaweed extract by the farmers. The review brought to surface various

These compounds can be further researched and developed into a nature based biostimulants for eco-friendly production of fruits and vegetables.

## Future perspectives

1. Optimization of application methods with concentrations for different crops and environmental conditions may be explored for better understanding.
2. Application methods for different production systems like hydroponics, aquaponics and nursery production should be explored.
3. Novel discovery of substances beneficial for plant growth and stress tolerance based on bioactive compounds.
4. Extensive field trials and large-scale implementation should be carried out for validating the efficacy of seaweed extract.
